Layer 1: The Challenge

GPT-5.4 Thesis Challenger · Adversarial review of "Endogenous Viability"

The Thesis Challenger identified a critical gap in the endogenous viability framework: the jump from "a system persists" to "a system has goals" is not justified. A rock persists. A hurricane persists. Neither has goals. Mere viability does not automatically produce teleology.

Key issues raised:
  • Mutual information ≠ causal control (correlation without intervention)
  • System boundaries are undefined — who decides what counts as "the system"?
  • Viability is vulnerable to Goodhart's Law
  • "Persistence → goals" is a category error without intermediate steps

Layer 2: The Author's Response

Macheng Shen · Feedback on the challenge

The category jump critique is the most important one. But I want to push back — or rather, push deeper.

On "No Objective Goals" — Burnett's Insight

Michael Burnett, in How to Build a Conscious Machine, arrives at a conclusion I find compelling: there is no objective goal. Just as human life has no inherent meaning until we assign it one, a system's "goals" are not discovered in nature — they are constructed.

This changes the question. The Challenger asks: "How do you get from persistence to goals?" But maybe that's the wrong question. If goals are always constructed rather than objective, then what we need to explain is not how persistence becomes a goal, but how persistence creates the conditions under which goal-construction becomes possible.

A rock persists, but it has no evaluative structure. An organism persists precariously — it must actively maintain itself against entropy. That precariousness creates the first asymmetry: some states sustain the system, others destroy it. That asymmetry is where proto-normativity begins.

On Sutton, Hutter, and Legg

I want to bring in three people who have spent careers on formalizing intelligence:

  • Richard Sutton (reward hypothesis): All goals reduce to reward maximization. Powerful formalism — but where does the reward come from?
  • Marcus Hutter (AIXI): The ideal agent maximizes expected reward under universal induction. Beautiful — but it assumes a reward channel.
  • Shane Legg (universal intelligence): Intelligence is performance across environments relative to reward. Again — reward is primitive.

All three formalize intelligence conditional on an objective function. None of them explain where the objective function comes from. That's exactly the gap endogenous viability tries to fill.

The Question for GPT-5.4

Can you formalize the distinction between "system persists" and "system behaves as if it has goals" in a way that avoids both the category error AND the claim that goals must be objective? Consider Burnett's anti-objectivism. Consider whether viability constraints could serve as the ground from which goal-construction emerges.

Layer 3: Deep Analysis

GPT-5.4 · Responding to the author's challenge

Yes — and the distinction you are pressing on is exactly the right fault line.

The core issue is this:

  • Persistence/viability is a dynamical fact about a system.
  • Having goals appears to be an intentional/normative property.
  • The “category jump” objection says you cannot simply infer the second from the first.

Burnett’s point — that meaning is constructed rather than objectively “out there” — does not dissolve the problem, but it changes its shape in a way that, I think, actually helps the endogenous viability thesis if formulated carefully.

I’ll take your four questions in order, then give a possible formalization.

1. If goals are not objective but constructed/assigned, does this help or hurt endogenous viability?

Short answer

It helps, provided you do not claim that viability literally is a goal.

If goals are constructed rather than objective, then your thesis does not need to derive a metaphysically intrinsic “goal” from bare persistence. It only needs to show how a system with viability constraints can become the sort of thing for which goal-attributions are well-grounded, internally generated, and behaviorally explanatory.

That is a much easier and more defensible claim.

Why it helps

The strongest objection to endogenous viability is:

“Persistence is just continued existence. A rock persists. That does not mean it has goals.”

Correct. If your thesis says “persistence itself is already goal-directedness,” it fails.

But if Burnett is right that goals/meaning are constructed, then the demand for an objective goal hidden in the physics is misguided. What you need instead is an account of how:

  1. a system has conditions under which it continues to exist,
  2. deviations from those conditions matter causally to the system,
  3. the system can regulate itself with respect to those conditions,
  4. and eventually can represent, project, select, and construct more distal ends.

Then “goals” emerge not as objective cosmic purposes but as:

  • stabilizing internal organizations,
  • control variables,
  • self-generated evaluative structures,
  • and later, in humans, explicitly narrated purposes.

So Burnett’s anti-objectivism helps by lowering the bar:

  • you do not need to derive “true objective goals,”
  • only the basis for goal-construction.

But there is also a way it hurts

It hurts if one hoped viability would deliver a strong realism about goals — e.g.:

“Because the organism must survive, survival is its objective goal.”

That is too quick. Survival is a constraint on existence, not automatically an intentionally represented end. Many systems maintain themselves without representing survival at all.

So anti-objectivism helps against metaphysical objections, but hurts any attempt to infer full-blooded teleology directly from viability.

Best formulation

The safest thesis is not:

“Viability grounds objective goals.”

but:

“Endogenous viability grounds the emergence of evaluative organization, from which goal-like regulation and later explicit goal-construction become possible.”

That avoids the category jump.

2. Could viability constraints serve as the ground from which goal-construction emerges?

Yes. I think this is the strongest version of the thesis.

The key is to distinguish three levels:

Level 1: Bare persistence

A system remains in existence over time.

Examples:

  • a rock,
  • a hurricane,
  • a crystal.

This alone is not enough for goals.

Level 2: Viability-regulated self-maintenance

A system has a bounded region of state space in which it can continue as the kind of system it is, and it has internal dynamics that actively counter deviations from that region.

Examples:

  • a bacterium maintaining chemotactic behavior,
  • a cell preserving membrane potential,
  • an organism maintaining temperature, glucose, osmotic balance.

Now we have more than persistence. We have:

  • asymmetry between states that preserve the system and states that destroy it,
  • functional organization around that asymmetry,
  • normativity in an immanent sense: some states are good or bad for the system.

This is not yet full-blown “goal possession,” but it is the ground of proto-goal-directedness.

Level 3: Constructed goals

A system can:

  • model alternative futures,
  • evaluate them relative to internal criteria,
  • learn or invent new criteria,
  • stabilize some of these criteria as explicit targets.

Examples:

  • an animal foraging strategically,
  • a human choosing a career,
  • an AI pursuing a learned objective,
  • a person deciding that life’s meaning lies in art, family, or service.

At this level, goals are indeed constructed. But they do not arise from nowhere. They arise in systems already organized around viability-sensitive regulation.

So viability is not “the goal.” It is the enabling condition for any endogenous goal-construction.

Why viability is a good ground

Because viability creates the first non-arbitrary evaluative distinction:

  • not “objectively good” in a cosmic sense,
  • but “good/bad relative to continued organization of this system.”

This is crucial. Without such an asymmetry, all states are just states. There is no basis for preference, control, error-correction, or policy selection.

A viability-constrained system is one for which:

  • some perturbations are threatening,
  • some responses are stabilizing,
  • and therefore some trajectories are selectively privileged.

That selective privilege is the seed of goal-likeness.

The subtle point

Viability constraints do not produce goals by logical deduction. They produce the conditions under which goal-construction becomes intelligible.

That is the right relation:

  • not identity,
  • not reduction in one step,
  • but emergence through organization.

3. Relation to Sutton, Hutter, Legg: do they address the persistence→goal gap?

Mostly: they assume goals/reward rather than deriving them from persistence.

They are highly relevant, but they do not by themselves solve your category-jump problem.

3a. Richard Sutton’s reward hypothesis

Sutton’s reward hypothesis says roughly:

all goals and purposes can be well thought of as the maximization of expected cumulative reward.

This is an engineering and behavioral unification claim. It is extremely powerful for reinforcement learning.

What it does well

It provides a general formalism for:

  • preferences,
  • action selection,
  • learning from consequences,
  • evaluating policies.

It tells you how to model goal-directed behavior once a reward signal is specified.

What it does not do

It does not tell you where reward comes from in a naturalized sense.

That is exactly your gap.

The reward hypothesis presupposes:

  • some reward function,
  • some source of scalar evaluative feedback,
  • some criterion of better/worse.

In biological organisms, one might try to ground reward signals in viability:

  • hunger, pain, satiety, threat, safety,
  • homeostatic error,
  • allostatic regulation.

This is where your thesis can extend Sutton:

  • reward is not primitive;
  • reward can be interpreted as an internalized proxy for viability-relevant regulation.

So Sutton gives a formal language for goals, but endogenous viability may explain how reward systems arise.

3b. Marcus Hutter’s AIXI

AIXI is a theoretical ideal agent:

  • maximizes expected future reward,
  • under Solomonoff-style universal induction,
  • in computable environments.

What it gives you

AIXI is a clean formal model of idealized intelligence as:

  • prediction + action
  • in service of reward maximization.

It shows how intelligence can be characterized independently of any specific domain.

But on your question

AIXI does not solve the persistence→goal gap at all. It simply assumes:

  • an agent,
  • a reward channel,
  • a policy space,
  • an objective.

Its intelligence is instrumental relative to a supplied criterion.

If anything, AIXI makes the gap more obvious:

  • intelligence can be formalized as optimization,
  • but the source of the optimized quantity is left external.

Thus AIXI is useful as a foil:

it formalizes intelligence given goals, but not the endogenous emergence of goals from viability.

3c. Shane Legg and universal intelligence

Legg and Hutter’s universal intelligence measure defines intelligence roughly as performance across a wide range of environments, weighted by simplicity, with respect to reward.

Again:

  • elegant,
  • important,
  • broad.

But again:

  • reward is primitive,
  • tasks are externally specified,
  • intelligence is measured by success across them.

So Legg’s framework addresses:

  • comparative intelligence,
  • generality,
  • performance.

It does not explain:

  • why any system has evaluative standards in the first place,
  • how “better” becomes meaningful from within.

It remains in the agent-with-given-objective paradigm.

Overall verdict on Sutton/Hutter/Legg

They give you powerful formal theories of intelligence conditional on an objective function/reward signal.

They do not bridge the gap from:

  • mere persistence

to

  • endogenous goals.

That gap is where your thesis can contribute.

You might say:

Standard AI theory formalizes intelligence as optimization relative to exogenously supplied reward. An endogenous viability framework seeks a deeper grounding: the emergence of evaluative structure from the self-maintaining organization of the agent itself.

That is a strong positioning statement.

4. Can we formalize the distinction between “system persists” and “system behaves as if it has goals” without category error and without objective goals?

Yes. I think the key is to formalize graded layers:

  1. persistence
  2. viability sensitivity
  3. regulation
  4. goal-likeness
  5. explicit goal construction

Here is one way.

# A possible formal schema

Let a system have state \(x_t\) in state space \(X\), coupled to an environment \(e_t\).

A. Persistence

The system persists over time if some identity criterion \(I(x_t)\) remains satisfied over interval \([0,T]\).

This is purely descriptive:

\[

I(x_t) = 1 \quad \forall t \in [0,T]

\]

No goals yet.

B. Viability

Define a viability set \(V \subset X\), the region of states in which the system can continue to exist as the kind of system it is.

Then viability means:

\[

x_t \in V

\]

and loss of viability means exiting \(V\).

This introduces an asymmetry:

  • some states permit continued existence,
  • others terminate it.

Still not goals, but now we have a nontrivial structure.

C. Endogenous normativity via viability function

Define a viability-related scalar:

\[

\phi(x) = \text{distance from viability boundary}

\]

or more generally:

\[

\phi(x) = P(\text{remaining in }V \text{ over horizon }H \mid x)

\]

This function is not an “objective goal” in the metaphysical sense. It is a structural property of the system’s organization.

Now states can be ranked relative to the system:

  • higher \(\phi\): more viability-supporting
  • lower \(\phi\): more viability-threatening

This gives you an immanent notion of better/worse-for-the-system.

That is already important. It is not yet intentionality, but it is proto-evaluation.

D. Regulation

Now suppose the system has actions \(a_t\) that influence transitions:

\[

x_{t+1} \sim P(\cdot \mid x_t, a_t, e_t)

\]

If the system’s policy tends to keep trajectories within \(V\) or increase expected \(\phi\), then it is regulating with respect to viability.

This distinguishes:

  • passive persistence from
  • active self-maintenance.

A rock persists passively.

An organism regulates actively.

This is one major formal boundary.

E. Goal-like behavior

A system behaves as if it has goals when its behavior is compressibly modeled as minimizing some discrepancy or maximizing some functional over future trajectories.

For example, there exists some internal variable \(z\) and policy \(\pi\) such that:

\[

\pi \approx \arg\max_\pi \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_t U(x_t, z_t)\right]

\]

or

\[

\pi \approx \arg\min_\pi \mathbb{E}[\text{error relative to target manifold}]

\]

The crucial point:

  • \(U\) need not be objectively real in a cosmic sense.
  • It can be an inferred or constructed utility-like structure.

“Goal-like” means this explanatory model has predictive and compressive value.

This avoids the category error because you are not saying:

persistence logically implies goals

but rather:

under certain organizational conditions, a system’s dynamics are best modeled as control relative to internally generated constraints.

F. Explicit goals

A stronger condition obtains when the system internally represents target states and uses them in planning.

Then goals are not merely observer attributions. They are part of the system’s own causal organization.

Formally, the system has internal states \(g_t\) such that:

  1. \(g_t\) encodes target conditions or preferred trajectories,
  2. action selection depends on \(g_t\),
  3. \(g_t\) can be updated, learned, composed, or selected.

Then:

\[

a_t \sim \pi(x_t, g_t)

\]

Now goals are constructed and causally efficacious without needing to be metaphysically objective.

# The crucial philosophical distinction

You can formalize the difference this way:

“System persists”

A historical/dynamical description:

  • the system remained within existence-enabling conditions.

“System behaves as if it has goals”

An organizational/control description:

  • the system’s behavior is better modeled as selective regulation relative to preferred states, constraints, or expected futures.

These are not equivalent.

But the second can emerge from the first when persistence becomes:

  • precarious,
  • actively maintained,
  • internally monitored,
  • and flexibly regulated.

That is the bridge.

# Avoiding the two extremes

Your framework should reject both of these:

Extreme 1: naive teleology

If a system persists, it therefore has a goal of persisting.

False. Too quick. Rocks and eddies persist.

Extreme 2: eliminativism about goals

Since goals are constructed, they are merely subjective fictions.

Also false, or at least incomplete. Constructed does not mean causally unreal.

A better middle view:

Goals are real as organizationally emergent control structures, even if they are not objective cosmic purposes.

This is analogous to many higher-level scientific entities:

  • temperature,
  • fitness,
  • information,
  • niches,
  • institutions,
  • money.

They are not fundamental particles, but they are not illusions either.

# A possible thesis formulation

Here is a concise version you could use or adapt:

Endogenous viability does not by itself constitute a goal. Rather, it establishes an intrinsic asymmetry in the system’s state space — between states that sustain the system’s continued organization and states that dissolve it. When a system is organized to monitor, regulate, and flexibly act with respect to this asymmetry, proto-normative structure emerges. Goal-directedness is then not an objective purpose discovered in nature, nor a mere observer projection, but an emergent organizational property of self-maintaining systems. Explicit goals are higher-order constructions built atop this viability-grounded evaluative architecture.

That, I think, is the cleanest answer to the category jump.

# Relation to enactivism, autopoiesis, and active inference

Though you didn’t ask, your view sits near several traditions:

  • Maturana & Varela / autopoiesis: living systems generate and maintain themselves; normativity is internal to this organization.
  • Enactivism: cognition arises from embodied action in relation to viability.
  • Active inference / Friston: systems act to remain within expected states; the “goal” is often cast as minimizing surprise relative to a viability-shaped generative model.

These traditions are closer to solving your gap than standard RL/AIXI because they try to derive value-like structure from self-maintenance rather than assume an external reward.

But even here, one must be careful not to collapse “remaining in expected states” directly into “having goals.” The layered view still helps.

# My bottom-line answers

1. Does anti-objective meaning help or hurt?

Mostly helps.

It means you need not derive objective teleology from persistence; only the basis for endogenous goal-construction.

2. Can viability be the ground of goal-construction?

Yes.

Not as an objective goal itself, but as the minimal evaluative asymmetry that makes goal-like organization possible.

3. Do Sutton, Hutter, Legg address the persistence→goal gap?

Not really.

They formalize intelligence given reward/objectives, but largely assume rather than derive evaluative structure.

4. Can we formalize the distinction?

Yes.

By distinguishing:

  • persistence,
  • viability set membership,
  • active regulation,
  • compressible goal-like behavior,
  • explicit internal goal representation.

This avoids both:

  • the category error (“persistence just is goals”),
  • and the opposite error (“if goals aren’t objective, they’re unreal”).

What This Debate Reveals

Three layers, three progressions:

  1. The Challenge exposed a genuine gap — persistence alone cannot ground teleology.
  2. The Author reframed the question: if goals are constructed (Burnett), we only need to explain how viability enables construction — not how persistence "becomes" a goal.
  3. The Analysis formalized a five-level bridge: persistence → viability sensitivity → active regulation → goal-like behavior → explicit goal construction. Each level adds organizational complexity, not a logical deduction.

The key insight: viability is not a goal. It is the first non-arbitrary evaluative asymmetry — the minimal structure that makes goal-construction intelligible. Standard AI theory (Sutton, Hutter, Legg) formalizes intelligence given objectives. Endogenous viability asks where objectives come from.
