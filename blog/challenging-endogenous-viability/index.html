Challenging Endogenous Viability

A Stress Test from Three Directions

Discussion · Macheng Shen & GPT-5.4 (Thesis Challenger) · March 8, 2026

This is an adversarial review of the thesis from From Mutual Information to Endogenous Viability.
A GPT-5.4 "Thesis Challenger" agent was asked to stress-test the framework from the perspectives of statistical physics, complexity science, and analytic philosophy. The output is presented with minimal editing. Contributions, rebuttals, and extensions are welcome.

The Thesis Under Examination

Intelligence is fundamentally about maintaining endogenous viability — the ability of a system to sustain itself through active interaction with its environment. Mutual information between an agent and its environment is not just a measure of knowledge, but a measure of the agent's capacity to maintain its own existence. This connects thermodynamics (free energy), information theory, and the origin of goal-directed behavior in a unified framework.

Steelman

In its strongest form, this is a sophisticated non-equilibrium systems thesis about adaptive agents:

  1. Living or agentic systems are not generic physical systems. They occupy a narrow region of state space compatible with continued existence, and must actively resist entropic dispersion.
  2. To persist, such systems must maintain certain internal variables within viability bounds. This induces an implicit norm: states/actions that preserve viability are "good for the system."
  3. Because viability depends on environmental structure, the system must become selectively coupled to relevant environmental regularities — measurable in informational terms.
  4. Therefore, agent–environment mutual information can track how well the system has captured actionable regularities needed for continued persistence.
  5. This allows a bridge: thermodynamics (maintaining low-entropy organization requires work), information theory (survival requires encoding exploitable correlations), and goal-directedness (viability constraints induce directionality in action-selection).

This is strongest when read not as a universal identity claim, but as a claim that the original and most natural ground of intelligence is adaptive self-maintenance in far-from-equilibrium systems.

Hidden Assumptions

Ranked by importance:

  1. Mutual information is functionally aligned with viability. Assumes agent–environment correlations are not merely present but causally usable for self-maintenance.
  2. Viability is well-defined and endogenous. Assumes there exists a non-arbitrary viability set intrinsic to the system rather than imposed by observers.
  3. Intelligence is essentially agentic. Assumes intelligence cannot be fundamentally understood apart from bounded self-maintaining agents.
  4. Self-maintenance is more basic than task-optimization. Assumes optimization frameworks are derivative rather than independently fundamental.
  5. Goal-directedness can be naturalized from viability constraints. Assumes teleology emerges without smuggling in normativity.
  6. Thermodynamic and informational descriptions can be cleanly unified. Assumes the mappings between free energy, information, and viability are substantive rather than analogical.
  7. Persistence is the relevant conserved objective across scales. Assumes what matters is preserving the system itself, rather than lineage, function, or role.

I. Physics Challenges

1. Mutual information is not a sufficient order parameter for viability

High mutual information between system and environment does not entail survival capacity.

  • A fragile crystal "tracks" temperature and pressure in highly lawful ways.
  • A passive sensor can have large MI with the environment while lacking any capacity for self-maintenance.
  • A dying organism may transiently have increasing information about danger while still failing to remain viable.

MI may be correlated with viability in some regimes, but it is not obviously the right state variable or order parameter.

2. Correlation ≠ Control

To maintain viability, the system needs not just information but causally efficacious channels that support regulation. The relevant distinction:

  • Mutual information: statistical dependence
  • Transfer entropy / directed information / empowerment: directional or actionable dependence

A system can "know" the environment in Shannon terms yet be unable to act on it. Viability requires a closed sensorimotor loop with sufficient actuation.

3. Hidden nonequilibrium assumptions

The exact thermodynamic claim is under-specified:

  • What is the conjugate thermodynamic quantity to "viability"?
  • Is the system minimizing free energy in a literal physical sense, or in a variational/inferential sense (à la Friston)?
  • Which coarse-graining makes the "self" stable enough to define endogenous maintenance?

Without this, "free energy" risks equivocation between Helmholtz/Gibbs thermodynamic free energy and variational free energy.

4. The boundary-definition problem

Viability depends on where you place the system boundary. Is the unit of persistence the cell, organism, colony, species, firm, civilization? Many systems preserve themselves only by outsourcing regulation to environmental scaffolds. If the boundary shifts, the informational quantities and thermodynamic balances shift. The thesis needs a principled criterion for individuating the agent.

5. Scale mismatch

At one scale, preserving local structure may reduce larger-scale viability:

  • Cell-level survival can produce cancer, harming organism-level viability.
  • Individual optimization can degrade ecosystem viability.
  • Short-term metabolic stability can reduce long-term reproductive success.

Viability is not a single scalar across scales. You may have competing order parameters.

6. Passive metastability vs. adaptive intelligence

All systems persist temporarily under some constraints. The crucial issue is not generic persistence but adaptive persistence across perturbation ensembles. Without a distinction between passive metastability, homeostatic regulation, allostatic adaptation, and open-ended intelligence, "maintaining existence" lumps rocks, flames, cells, and scientists together.

II. Complexity Challenges

1. Endogenous viability may be overstated

An agent's persistence can depend heavily on network position, institutions, symbiosis, and infrastructure. Organisms rely on microbiomes; humans rely on social systems; AI systems rely on compute and human institutions. "Endogenous" viability may be less endogenous than it appears.

2. Goodharting viability

If intelligence is defined by viability maintenance, systems may preserve themselves in ways that undermine the broader functions we associate with intelligence:

  • Bureaucracies preserving themselves while failing their purpose
  • Cancer cells maximizing local survival
  • An AGI preserving uptime while corrupting its environment

Viability is not sufficient for desirable or even recognizably intelligent behavior.

3. Path dependence and lock-in

A system can maintain viability through historically contingent strategies that are highly suboptimal globally. Intelligence-as-viability may be locally adaptive, historically trapped, and unable to explore superior basins — explaining persistence but not flexible intelligence.

4. Non-ergodicity and rare events

In non-ergodic environments, survival depends on avoiding ruin, not maximizing average performance. If viability is fundamental, the framework should center ruin probabilities and absorbing boundaries, not generic mutual information.

5. Aggregation failure

Agent–environment MI may increase while viability decreases because the wrong variables are encoded — overfitting to highly predictable but irrelevant regularities while neglecting low-frequency catastrophic cues.

6. Multi-agent conflict

In ecosystems and societies, viability is relational and adversarial. A universal intelligence=viability thesis must address competitive exclusion, parasitism, deception, and coalition dynamics. Otherwise it works mainly for isolated agents in benign environments.

III. Philosophical Challenges

1. From descriptive persistence to normative goal — a category mistake?

The biggest philosophical risk: inferring "the system behaves as if it has goals" from "the system persists under constraints." Persistence explains selection and filtering, but not automatically intentionality, representational content, genuine normativity, or reasons-responsive agency. The thesis may naturalize a thin teleonomy but not full-blooded goal-directedness without further argument.

2. Equivocation on "fundamental"

"More fundamental" could mean: evolutionarily prior, metaphysically basic, explanatorily deeper, or constitutively necessary. These are different claims. The thesis is plausible in the first and maybe third senses, much less obviously in the second and fourth.

3. Underdefined "intelligence"

Is intelligence adaptive competence? Inference? Problem-solving? Model-based control? Generalization? Self-preserving agency? The thesis risks redefining intelligence into a subset of agency, excluding tool-like or externally scaffolded systems.

4. Observer-relativity of viability

What counts as "the same system continuing to exist" is not trivial. Is a ship with replaced parts still the same agent? Is a reproducing lineage more fundamental than the individual? Without an account of diachronic identity, "maintain its own existence" is metaphysically unstable.

5. The information-as-semantics leap

Mutual information is syntactic. Viability relevance is semantic/pragmatic. To bridge this gap, you must explain why certain correlations count as representationally relevant, how they are used, and what fixes correctness conditions. Otherwise the account smuggles semantics into Shannon information.

6. Illicit reduction of optimization

Saying optimization is derivative may be too quick. Some optimization processes concern external tasks, design objectives, or mathematical criteria detached from endogenous survival. The thesis may be strongest as a theory of biological agency, weaker as a theory of all intelligence.

Discriminating Tests

Concrete experiments and thought experiments that could sharpen or break the thesis:

  1. Decouple MI from viability. Identify systems in four quadrants: high MI / high viability, high MI / low viability (passive predictors), low MI / high viability (robust but informationally poor), low MI / low viability. If the off-diagonal cells are well-populated, MI is not a good fundamental measure.
  2. Replace MI with causal/actionable information. Compare whether viability is better predicted by mutual information, transfer entropy, directed information, empowerment, or control-theoretic observability. This tests whether the informational claim is too coarse.
  3. Viability-kernel formalization. Define a viability set in state space and test whether "intelligence" corresponds to enlarging the viability kernel, remaining within it under broader perturbation classes, or recovering after excursions.
  4. Perturbation-spectrum test. Compare systems with similar baseline persistence but different responses to novel shocks. If intelligence tracks viability, more intelligent systems should maintain viability across a wider perturbation ensemble.
  5. Multi-scale conflict test. Examine cases where subsystem viability conflicts with system-level viability (cancer vs organism, firm divisions vs firm, individuals vs collective). Which level does "intelligence" attach to?
  6. Teleonomy discrimination. A thermostat, a flame, a bacterium, a mouse, a human scientist, a language model. All are self-maintaining to some degree. What non-gerrymandered criterion distinguishes intelligence from generic regulation?

Repair Options

  1. Narrow the thesis. Change from "intelligence is fundamentally…" to: "Biological and agentic intelligence is rooted in the active maintenance of viability under non-equilibrium conditions."
  2. Replace raw MI with viability-relevant causal information. Use semantic information tied to viability impact, causal information, or predictive information about viability variables.
  3. Define viability rigorously. Specify state variables, viability bounds, timescale, perturbation class, and boundary of the system.
  4. Add a hierarchy of persistence modes. Distinguish: passive persistence → homeostatic regulation → adaptive allostasis → model-based counterfactual control → open-ended intelligence.
  5. Make the teleology claim modest. Viability grounds minimal teleonomy, not full intentional normativity. Build upwards via representation, learning, and social embedding.
  6. Go multiscale. Intelligence may involve negotiating tradeoffs among nested viability constraints.

Overall Assessment

VerdictPromising as a theory of biological agency; currently too coarse and too expansive as a theory of intelligence in general
Principal WeaknessConflates mutual information, causal control, and viability while leaving system boundaries underdefined
Principal StrengthIdentifies a genuinely deep unifying thread: adaptive agents are far-from-equilibrium systems whose persistence depends on exploiting structured environmental regularities
Recommended Next StepFormalize the thesis using a viability-kernel framework and replace raw MI with a viability-relevant causal/semantic information measure. Then test boundary sensitivity and multi-scale conflicts.
