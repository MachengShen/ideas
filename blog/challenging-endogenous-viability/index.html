Challenging Endogenous Viability

A Stress Test from Three Directions

Discussion · Macheng Shen & GPT-5.4 (Thesis Challenger) · March 8, 2026

Adversarial review of From Mutual Information to Endogenous Viability.
GPT-5.4 Thesis Challenger. Minimal editing.

Thesis Summary

The thesis argues for a progression:

  1. Raw mutual information between agent and world is insufficient as an account of representation or intelligence.
  2. What matters is not information per se but task-relevant information: distinctions that support prediction, control, planning, transfer, survival, etc.
  3. But “task relevance” itself is usually taken as primitive in ML, imported via labels, reward functions, benchmarks, or externally chosen objectives.
  4. Therefore the deeper problem is to explain where relevance comes from internally rather than stipulating it from outside.
  5. The title suggests the proposed destination: endogenous viability—roughly, the idea that relevance is grounded in what matters for the system’s continued functioning/existence, not in exogenous task definitions.

So the core move is from:

  • representation = high mutual information with world

to

  • representation = information organized around what sustains the system’s own viability

Steelman

In its strongest form, the thesis is not merely criticizing mutual information. It is making a structural claim about intelligence:

  • A representation is not just a correlated internal state.
  • Nor is it simply a compressed predictive state.
  • It is a selective internal organization that preserves world-structure in virtue of its role in maintaining the agent’s adaptive success across time.

The best charitable reconstruction is:

  1. MI is necessary but radically non-diagnostic.

Correlation alone cannot distinguish representation from passive registration, memorization, noise capture, or epiphenomenal coupling.

  1. Usefulness requires a criterion of selective retention.

An intelligent system cannot preserve all environmental variation; it must compress while preserving distinctions that matter downstream.

  1. Downstream utility in ML is often externally defined.

Labels, rewards, and benchmark tasks hide the real issue by treating relevance as given.

  1. A genuine theory of representation in autonomous systems needs an endogenous grounding of relevance.

That grounding is plausibly the system’s own continued viability, stability, or self-maintenance under environmental perturbation.

  1. Thus representation is tied to adaptive organization, not just information transmission.

Internal states represent the world insofar as they encode distinctions whose tracking contributes to preserving the system’s viable regime.

That is a serious and potentially powerful thesis. It would connect information theory, control, autonomy, and biological/agentic organization.

Hidden Assumptions (ranked by importance)

  1. Viability can serve as a sufficiently general endogenous objective.

This is the lynchpin. It assumes survival/self-maintenance can ground relevance for intelligent systems broadly, including artificial ones.

  1. There is a principled distinction between mere covariance and representation.

The thesis assumes this distinction can be made non-circularly and formally.

  1. Task relevance is underived in standard ML rather than merely operationalized.

This is partly true, but some frameworks derive relevance from dynamics, interventions, or control constraints rather than importing it naively.

  1. A system’s continued viability is identifiable at the right level of description.

Viability may be obvious for organisms, but less so for distributed institutions, models, or software agents.

  1. Representation must be tied to agent-relative function rather than observer-relative description.

That is plausible but philosophically loaded.

  1. There exists a useful decomposition between agent internal state and external world state.

This boundary may itself be fluid, negotiated, or theory-relative.

  1. The relevant notion of intelligence is fundamentally adaptive and embodied.

This may exclude mathematically intelligent but non-self-maintaining systems.

Physics Challenges

1. No conserved quantity has yet been identified

The thesis pivots from MI to viability, but viability is not yet a clear physical quantity. Mutual information is formally defined. Viability is often not.

Challenge:

  • What exactly is the state variable, order parameter, or constrained manifold corresponding to “viability”?
  • Is it a region in state space?
  • A free-energy budget?
  • A metastable basin?
  • A homeostatic invariant set?

Without this, the move risks being from a precise but insufficient quantity to an evocative but underformalized one.

2. Boundary dependence is doing major hidden work

Inside/outside distinction is treated as natural. In physics it is often not.

Questions:

  • What individuates the agent boundary?
  • If internal representation is defined by mutual information with “external states,” what stops us from redescribing the coupled agent-environment system differently?
  • Is viability invariant under coarse-graining or repartition of degrees of freedom?

If the theory depends strongly on a chosen decomposition, it may be observer-relative rather than intrinsic.

3. Equilibrium assumptions may be smuggled in

The thesis treats relevance as tied to future consequences. But in non-equilibrium systems, “future usefulness” depends on path-dependent flows and transient regimes, not just stationary distributions.

Challenge:

  • Is viability defined for transient adaptation or only near steady states?
  • Are you assuming ergodicity when saying the system must preserve distinctions “that matter for what comes next”?
  • Many intelligent systems operate in nonstationary environments where long-run averages are misleading.

A representation sufficient under one stationary regime may fail catastrophically across regime shifts.

4. Mutual information is coarse, but so is viability unless multiscale structure is specified

In physical systems, what matters often depends on scale:

  • molecular stability,
  • cellular regulation,
  • organismic survival,
  • reproductive fitness,
  • lineage persistence.

These can conflict. Which viability scale grounds representation?

If no scale is privileged, the account may be underdetermined. If one is privileged, justification is needed.

5. Causal asymmetry is missing

MI is symmetric. Representation is not. The world constrains the agent, and the agent uses internal states to guide action back onto the world.

A physics-grade account likely needs:

  • directed information,
  • transfer entropy,
  • intervention-sensitive state variables,
  • or control-theoretic sufficient statistics.

Otherwise the thesis correctly rejects MI, but may not yet replace it with the right asymmetric quantity.

Complexity Challenges

1. “Viability” may Goodhart against intelligence

If endogenous viability is the grounding criterion, a system can optimize viability in degenerate ways:

  • withdraw from exploration,
  • manipulate its own sensors,
  • narrow the environment,
  • become brittle but locally self-stabilizing,
  • preserve itself at the cost of world-model quality.

This creates a central failure mode: survival is not equivalent to intelligence.

A bacterium and a scientist are both viable. The former may need very little representation. So viability may be necessary for autonomy but insufficient for rich intelligence.

2. Path dependence and niche construction are underplayed

What is “relevant” is often not just discovered but actively created by the agent:

  • organisms modify niches,
  • institutions reshape environments,
  • AI systems alter data distributions through deployment.

Then task relevance is not simply endogenous in the sense of “internally grounded”; it is co-constituted by feedback loops between system and environment.

This matters because a representation may be useful only in the niche the agent itself has built. That weakens claims of general world-representation.

3. Multi-objective conflict is likely

Real systems do not optimize a single viability criterion. They trade off:

  • immediate survival,
  • reproduction,
  • exploration,
  • social coordination,
  • energy efficiency,
  • robustness,
  • optionality.

A single endogenous grounding variable may collapse important structure. Representation quality may depend on balancing conflicting objectives across timescales.

4. Aggregation failure: local utility does not imply global adequacy

A system may encode locally viability-relevant distinctions while missing global structure needed for long-horizon adaptation. Examples:

  • myopic heuristics,
  • overfit ecological specialization,
  • local homeostasis that causes long-term collapse.

So “useful for viability” must specify horizon length, perturbation class, and environmental family. Otherwise the thesis risks validating brittle short-term adaptation.

5. Regime shifts break endogenous criteria

Representations shaped by one viability regime can become anti-viable under shocks. This implies that relevance is not an intrinsic property of variables alone but of variables relative to a distribution over futures.

That means endogenous viability still needs a reference class of environments. Where does that come from?

Philosophical Challenges

1. Possible equivocation on “representation”

The essay appears to move among at least three notions:

  • statistical correlation,
  • functional sufficiency for task performance,
  • semantic aboutness of world states.

These are not identical. Showing that MI is insufficient for the first does not yet explain the third. Viability may ground function, but function does not automatically yield semantics.

This is the classic problem: how do we get from “state helps survival” to “state represents predator presence”?

2. Normativity may be reintroduced, not eliminated

You criticize exogenous “task” as imported normativity. But viability itself is normative: some states count as better because they preserve the system.

Why is viability less “smuggled in” than reward? It may be more natural for organisms, but for many artificial systems it is also imposed by design.

So the thesis may replace one source of normativity with another without fully justifying the transition.

3. Category risk: intelligence vs autonomy vs agency

The argument may be strongest as a theory of autonomous adaptive agents, not of intelligence simpliciter.

A theorem prover, a language model, or a passive scientific instrument may possess sophisticated world-involving structure without endogenous self-maintenance being central. If so, the thesis overgeneralizes.

4. Hidden teleology

“Useful,” “for the sake of,” and “relevance” can conceal teleological assumptions. If those are cashed out functionally, fine. But then you owe an account of function:

  • etiological?
  • systemic?
  • selected-effect?
  • design-based?

Without that, viability may look like a teleological primitive.

5. Modal ambiguity

Relevant to which future? Good question. But the answer cannot simply be “the futures in which the system remains viable” unless modal space is specified:

  • nearby counterfactuals?
  • all physically possible perturbations?
  • historically sampled environments?

Different modal bases produce different relevance criteria.

Crosscutting Failures

1. Under-specification of the target quantity

The essay successfully destabilizes MI but has not yet provided a replacement quantity with comparable rigor. The danger is a rhetorical gradient:

  • precise but inadequate concept rejected,
  • richer but vaguer concept proposed.

2. Failure to separate necessity from sufficiency

Likely true:

  • MI is necessary for many forms of adaptive representation.
  • Task relevance is more diagnostic than raw MI.
  • Viability may ground some forms of relevance.

Not yet shown:

  • viability is sufficient to explain representation,
  • or that all intelligence-relevant representations derive from it.

3. Scale instability

The account risks changing truth value across levels:

  • neuron,
  • subsystem,
  • organism,
  • collective,
  • species,
  • artificial pipeline.

A representation can be viability-relevant at one level and irrelevant or harmful at another.

4. Observer-relativity risk

If agent boundaries, viability conditions, and task decompositions are chosen by the theorist, the “endogenous” turn may be less endogenous than advertised.

Discriminating Tests

1. Minimal counterexample test

Construct systems with high MI but no plausible representation:

  • a camera buffer,
  • a lossy compressor tuned to environmental statistics,
  • a giant lookup table.

Then compare to systems with lower MI but stronger adaptive utility:

  • predictive state abstractions for control,
  • compressed latent models with interventionally useful variables.

If your framework cannot sharply distinguish these, it has not advanced enough.

2. Viability dissociation test

Build two agents:

  • Agent A maximizes viability through rigid reflexes and environmental simplification.
  • Agent B builds richer latent models that support transfer and planning but has similar short-term viability.

Question: which one “represents” more?

If the answer tracks only viability, the account is too weak. If it tracks model structure beyond viability, specify the extra criterion.

3. Regime-shift robustness test

Train/shape representations in one environment, then evaluate under distributional shifts:

  • novel perturbations,
  • delayed effects,
  • hidden-state changes.

If viability-grounded relevance leads to robust latent variables, that supports the thesis. If it overfits the local niche, viability is too parochial.

4. Boundary perturbation test

Redefine system boundaries and coarse-grainings:

  • include memory aids as part of agent,
  • treat sensorimotor loop as extended system,
  • partition internal subsystems differently.

Does the account of representation and relevance remain stable? If not, the theory depends too much on observer choice.

5. Intervention-based semantics test

For candidate internal variable \(Z\), test:

  • Does intervening on world variable \(W\) systematically alter \(Z\)?
  • Does intervening on \(Z\) alter action and downstream viability in ways mediated by \(W\)?
  • Is \(Z\) a sufficient statistic for a class of control-relevant counterfactuals?

This would move from correlation to causal-functional representation.

6. Timescale discrimination test

Evaluate whether the same representation supports:

  • immediate homeostasis,
  • medium-horizon control,
  • long-horizon adaptation.

If not, viability is not one thing; it is a hierarchy. The theory must reflect that.

Repair Options

1. Replace “viability” with a structured viability manifold

Make viability explicit as:

  • a bounded region in state space,
  • with timescale-indexed constraints,
  • under a distribution over perturbations,
  • relative to a system boundary.

This gives the concept physical traction.

2. Move from mutual information to causal/invariant information

Instead of MI alone, use concepts such as:

  • interventionally sufficient latent variables,
  • predictive representations under action,
  • directed information,
  • empowerment/control-theoretic sufficiency,
  • invariants across environmental transformations.

That would better capture representation as usable structure rather than raw correlation.

3. Distinguish three levels of representation

Be explicit:

  1. Correlational representation: mere statistical dependence.
  2. Functional representation: supports prediction/control under counterfactuals.
  3. Semantic representation: world-directed content under success conditions.

Then claim endogenous viability grounds level 2, and perhaps partially explains level 3, without conflating them.

4. Restrict scope

State clearly that the thesis targets autonomous embodied agents, not all intelligent systems. That would make it stronger by avoiding overreach.

5. Add multiscale viability

Define relevance relative to:

  • short-term homeostasis,
  • medium-term competence,
  • long-term adaptability/open-ended viability.

This avoids validating brittle, myopic survival strategies.

6. Include niche construction

Endogenize relevance not only via self-maintenance but via agent-environment co-organization. Representation is for maintaining viability in a niche the agent partly stabilizes and partly explores.

Overall Assessment

Verdict: Promising and directionally correct, but not yet theoretically closed.

Principal weakness: The replacement for mutual information—endogenous viability—remains underdefined and may be too weak, too boundary-dependent, and too parochial to ground representation on its own.

Principal strength: It identifies the real bottleneck: “task relevance” is often treated as primitive, and any deep theory of representation must explain where relevance comes from rather than importing it from labels or rewards.

Recommended next step: Formalize viability as a multiscale, perturbation-relative constraint set and define representation in causal-functional terms: an internal state represents external structure iff it is an interventionally sufficient compression of world dynamics for maintaining viability across a specified family of futures.
